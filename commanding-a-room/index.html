The Five Rules

Great speakers are not born — they are built. Every habit below can be learned, practised, and owned. Work through one rule at a time and you will feel the shift in how rooms respond to you.

1
Rule One Own Your Entrance

The room forms its opinion in the first eight seconds. Before you say a single word, you are already communicating. Walk in with intention — shoulders back, pace measured, eyes scanning the room as if you already belong there. Because you do.

Rushing, fidgeting, or looking at your phone as you enter signals anxiety and shrinks your authority before you have spoken. A commanding entrance is simply a deliberate one.

Practice drill

Before your next presentation, pause for three full seconds at the entrance point. Breathe. Then walk. Notice how the energy shifts when you stop rushing.

2
Rule Two Silence Is Your Most Powerful Tool

Nervous speakers fill silence with words. Commanding speakers use silence as punctuation. A well-placed pause after a key point gives your audience time to absorb it, signals that you are not rushing, and creates a magnetic sense of control.

The silence will feel far longer to you than it does to your audience. Two seconds of quiet feels like ten seconds when you are the one standing there — but to the room it reads as confidence.

Practice drill

After your strongest statement in any talk, count silently to three before continuing. Let the room sit with it. The discomfort you feel is the confidence the audience sees.

3
Rule Three Speak to One Person at a Time

Most speakers scan the room continuously, making eye contact with no one. Commanding speakers do the opposite — they hold eye contact with one individual for a complete thought (usually five to seven seconds), then move to another. This turns a presentation into a series of one-to-one conversations.

When someone in an audience feels personally seen, they lean in. And when one person leans in, the people around them notice — and follow.

Practice drill

In your next talk, pick three anchor points in the room — left, centre, right. Deliver complete sentences to each. Resist the urge to sweep. Finish the thought, then move.

4
Rule Four Lower Your Register Under Pressure

When we are nervous, our voice rises in pitch, speeds up, and loses its resonance. This is the voice of someone being chased. To command a room, train the opposite response — when the pressure rises, consciously slow down and drop your pitch.

A lower, slower voice signals safety and authority. It is also far easier to listen to. The audience relaxes, which relaxes you, which deepens your presence — a virtuous cycle that begins with a single intentional breath.

Practice drill

Record yourself reading one paragraph at your natural pace, then again at 70% of that speed. Listen back. The slower version will almost always sound more authoritative, not boring.

5
Rule Five Close with Conviction, Not a Question

How a speaker ends is what the audience remembers. Weak closings — "So… that's basically it," "Any questions?", "I think that's all I have" — dissolve the authority you built. They signal that you were not sure you had earned the room's time.

A commanding close is a statement, not an apology. Summarise your single most important point, say it with finality, and then stop talking. The silence after a strong closing line is not awkward — it is applause waiting to happen.

Practice drill

Write your closing line before you write anything else in your next talk. Make it one sentence. Make it declarative. Memorise it word for word and deliver it without looking at notes.

Your Five-Rule Checklist

Own your entrance — pause, then walk
Use silence as punctuation
Hold eye contact for full thoughts
Slow down and lower under pressure
Close with a statement, not a question
