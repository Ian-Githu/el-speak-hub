In June 2026, I ran a pilot: a small group, a day at Brackenhurst in Tigoni, and one goal, leave with an AI assistant that actually runs part of your week. No theory-only slides, no "AI 101." Everyone built something real and tested it before lunch.

It was free, because I wanted to get the format right before charging for it. It worked. Here's what came out of it, and what the next cohorts look like.

  • 01Start with one workflow, not ten. The biggest wins came from picking a single recurring task and automating that completely.
  • 02Give the assistant context, not just instructions. The setups that stuck were the ones built around how people actually think and work.
  • 03Confirm before anything irreversible. Every good build had a human checkpoint before sending, publishing, or deleting.
  • 04Connection still beats configuration. The best builds were the ones people could explain in plain language, and that's what kept them using it after.
"A perfect deck means nothing if the room isn't with you. The same is true for a perfect workflow. If it doesn't fit how you actually think, you won't use it."
